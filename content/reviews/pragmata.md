It would be easy to approach Pragmata with lowered expectations. Announced at Sony's original PS5 reveal in June 2020, the game racked up delay after delay, morphing from a mysterious 2022 launch window into a running joke about vaporware. And yet here we are — April 2026, the game is finally in our hands, and Capcom has done it again.

Pragmata puts you in the boots of Hugh, a space auditor stranded on a cold, malfunctioning lunar research station. Within minutes he crosses paths with Diana, a young android with the ability to hack technology in real time. That pairing — the gruff, disposable-weapon-wielding dad figure and the glowing-eyed kid who can puppet machines with a thought — becomes the game's beating heart, and it works beautifully.

The combat system is where Pragmata truly carves out its identity. You're constantly switching between standard third-person shooting and directing Diana's hacking abilities against enemies and environmental systems simultaneously. It sounds exhausting on paper. In practice, it creates a beautiful rhythm — one half of your brain tracking trajectory and cover, the other solving the short puzzle loops that bring shielded robots to their knees. It's the most fun I've had with a Capcom shooter since Resident Evil 4's remake, and this isn't even technically a Resident Evil game.

The RE Engine continues to be one of the most versatile tools in games. Pragmata's corrupted recreation of New York — deliberately glitched out and subtly wrong in the AI-generated way the devs intentionally baked in — is a standout environment, unsettling without being hostile. The lunar station sections are claustrophobic and beautifully lit. Frame rates were rock solid across PS5 and PC sessions, and the Switch 2 version holds up better than anyone had a right to expect.

The story is lean — about 12 to 15 hours on a standard playthrough — and unashamedly sentimental. Some will bounce off it; the emotional beats lean into father-daughter territory that Capcom plays absolutely straight-faced. But for those who let it land, the relationship between Hugh and Diana reaches genuinely surprising depth, particularly in the final third.

If there's a knock, it's that the arsenal of disposable weapons occasionally feels underdeveloped, and a handful of mid-game encounters feel like padding between the genuinely inspired set pieces. But these are minor complaints about a game that Steam users are currently rating 97% positive. Capcom set the bar high with RE Requiem this year. Somehow, they've come close to clearing it again.
