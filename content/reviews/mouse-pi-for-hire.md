Let's get the obvious out of the way: Mouse: P.I. For Hire is one of the most visually striking games of the year. Fumi Games' debut title renders an entire city in black-and-white 1930s rubber-hose animation — the same style that gave us early Mickey Mouse and the original Popeye cartoons. Enemies squash and stretch. Bullets are drawn in thick ink. Explosions bloom like comic panel splash pages. It is, genuinely, extraordinary to look at.

But this is also a game, not just a tech demo — and the good news is that the shooting underneath the paint job has more than enough substance to carry you through an 8-hour campaign. You play as Jack Pepper, a mouse detective investigating corruption in the city of Mouseburg, voiced with charismatic energy by Troy Baker. The arsenal reads like a 1930s crime film prop list: revolvers, tommy guns, Carcano rifles, and dynamite. Movement is fast, dodging is satisfying, and the Fantastic-o-Matic vending machine power-up system gives each run through a level its own flavour.

Combat channels the DNA of classic boomer shooters — think Doom Eternal's resource management and Wolfenstein's corridor-clearing momentum — but grounds it in a detective fiction structure that keeps chapters feeling distinct. The game is committed to the bit in a way that genuinely earns its Cuphead comparisons.

Where Mouse stumbles is in the marriage of its detective narrative and its shooter gameplay. The investigation elements — clue gathering, conversation snippets, some light environmental storytelling — feel surface-level against the depth of the action. The noir storyline is enjoyable but never rises above competent, which leaves the campaign feeling slightly lopsided: the gameplay earns a 9, the story earns a 7, and the average lands right around where the critical consensus sits.

The final section overstays its welcome — a notably less inventive stretch that arrives just as the earlier chapters had built up real momentum. It doesn't ruin the experience, but it means Mouse exits your memory on a slightly flat note rather than a roaring finale.

None of this changes the fundamental pitch: Mouse: P.I. For Hire is one of the best indie debuts in years, a passion project from a small studio that swung for a wildly ambitious visual language and connected. It launched the same day as Pragmata and still managed to carve out its own conversation. At $39.99, the risk-to-reward ratio is absolutely in your favour. Fumi Games has announced DLC is coming — if they use it to address the narrative shortcomings, this could be remembered as one of 2026's most underrated gems.
