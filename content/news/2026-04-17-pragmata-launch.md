# Capcom's Moon Mystery Is Finally Here — And It's Brilliant

There is a particular kind of anticipation that builds around a game that refuses to show its hand. When Capcom first unveiled **Pragmata** at Sony's original PS5 reveal event in June 2020, it gave us exactly 90 seconds: a man in a spacesuit standing in a glitching New York City, a small girl in an astronaut helmet, and a cut to black. That was it. No gameplay. No genre explanation. Just vibes, mystery, and a logo that meant nothing to anyone.

Six years, multiple delays, and what felt like an endless parade of "still coming, we promise" updates later, Pragmata is finally in players' hands — and the verdict is in. Capcom didn't just avoid the trap that swallows most long-delayed games. They cleared it by a mile.

## What Is Pragmata, Exactly?

If you've managed to avoid spoiling yourself on the details: Pragmata is a third-person sci-fi action game built on Capcom's RE Engine, the same technology powering the Resident Evil remakes and Monster Hunter Rise. You play as **Hugh**, a member of an investigation team dispatched to a lunar research station that has been taken over by rogue artificial intelligence. Within the first twenty minutes, Hugh crosses paths with **Diana**, a young android with the unusual ability to interface with and hack electronic systems in real time. The two are stuck together, and from that point the game becomes a story about two very different people — one human, one not — figuring out how to trust each other while the world falls apart around them.

The genre is harder to pin down. It's a third-person shooter in the same way that Resident Evil 4 is a third-person shooter — technically accurate, but not really the point. The hacking system that Diana enables transforms every combat encounter into a layered puzzle. Enemies have shields that can only be brought down through hacking mini-games. Environmental hazards can be turned into weapons. Systems can be overloaded to create distractions. You're constantly splitting your attention between the physical fight and the digital one happening simultaneously, and finding the rhythm between the two is what makes the combat genuinely unlike anything else on the market right now.

## Six Years of Waiting: Was It Worth It?

Short answer: yes, obviously.

Longer answer: the delays actually make more sense in retrospect. The game Capcom was building in 2020 is not the game that shipped in 2026. Early builds apparently leaned much harder into the horror DNA that the studio is known for — claustrophobic, tense, survival-adjacent. What shipped is warmer, faster, and more action-forward. The relationship between Hugh and Diana moved from a functional partnership to something approaching a genuine emotional core. The hacking system, which Capcom has said was rebuilt from scratch at least twice, became the mechanical heart of the whole thing rather than a side system bolted on for variety.

PC Gamer scored the finished product **87%**, describing it as "a snazzy late-aughts throwback elevated by a terrific sense of feedback and momentum." TheGamer gave it **4.5 out of 5**, with critic Jade King writing that it has "big ambitions, a big heart, and two big and beautiful characters leading the charge on an intergalactic adventure like no other." Metacritic aggregates currently sit between **85 and 88** depending on platform, putting it firmly among the highest-rated games of 2026. Only six titles have scored better on OpenCritic so far this year — and two of them are also Capcom games.

Steam users are even more enthusiastic. At time of writing, Pragmata holds a **97% positive** rating from nearly 7,000 user reviews, with players consistently praising the combat system and the character writing.

## Capcom's Unstoppable 2026

It's worth pausing to acknowledge what Capcom has quietly pulled off this year. Resident Evil Requiem launched in January to an 89 on Metacritic and became the fastest-selling entry in franchise history. Monster Hunter Stories 3: Twisted Reflection shipped in March to widespread critical acclaim. And now Pragmata arrives in April scoring in the high 80s on a brand new IP.

Three major releases. Three critical hits. In four months.

The studio still has Onimusha: Way of the Sword on the calendar before the year is out. Whether that continues the streak or finally breaks it, 2026 is already looking like a career-defining year for Capcom — the kind of run that gets talked about for a decade.

## The Bottom Line

Pragmata is a 12 to 15 hour single-player campaign that respects your time, delivers a story that earns its emotional moments, and houses a combat system that rewards mastery without punishing newcomers. The RE Engine ensures it's technically stunning on every platform it shipped on, and the Switch 2 version is a genuine surprise — running far better than anyone anticipated for a game of this visual complexity.

The disposable weapon system feels underdeveloped in the late game, and there's no New Game+ or post-game content at launch — which, given how strong the second half is, genuinely stings. DLC or a NG+ update would be very welcome.

None of that changes the fundamental verdict: **this is one of the best new IPs to debut this generation.** Buy it.

**Pragmata is available now on PS5, Xbox Series X|S, PC, and Switch 2. $59.99.**
